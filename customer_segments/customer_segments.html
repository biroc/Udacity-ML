Implementation: Outlier Detection¶

Detecting outliers in the data is extremely important in the data preprocessing step of any analysis. The presence of outliers can often skew results which take into consideration these data points. There are many "rules of thumb" for what constitutes an outlier in a dataset. Here, we will use Tukey's Method for identfying outliers: An outlier step is calculated as 1.5 times the interquartile range (IQR). A data point with a feature that is beyond an outlier step outside of the IQR for that feature is considered abnormal.

In the code block below, you will need to implement the following:

  • Optionally remove data points from the dataset by adding indices to the outliers list.

Question 6¶

What are the advantages to using a K-Means clustering algorithm? What are the advantages to using a Gaussian Mixture Model clustering algorithm? Given your observations about the wholesale customer data so far, which of the two algorithms will you use and why?

Answer:
The advantages of k-means are:

  • It is computationally cheap compared to the GMM, needing only to calculate the means of the points in each cluster instead of the parameters of a gaussian distribution
  • It is simple to implement, needing only a selection of k clusters
  • It always converges, and scales well to large data sets


The advantages of a gaussian mixture model are:

  • It allows soft (probabilistic) classification, where k-means requires classifications to be hard (e.g. 100%) cluster assignments
  • GMM is more flexible with cluster variances, allowing different gaussians to have different variances, where k-means attempts to split the data into groups of equal variance


Given our customer data problem, k-means seems to be the best fit. The chief advantage of a GMM is its probabilistic (fuzzy) clustering, and in our case we are seeking to provide hard cluster assignments as is done in k-means. For this reason, we should use k-means here.

Implementation: Creating Clusters¶

Depending on the problem, the number of clusters that you expect to be in the data may already be known. When the number of clusters is not known a priori, there is no guarantee that a given number of clusters best segments the data, since it is unclear what structure exists in the data — if any. However, we can quantify the "goodness" of a clustering by calculating each data point's silhouette coefficient. The silhouette coefficient for a data point measures how similar it is to its assigned cluster from -1 (dissimilar) to 1 (similar). Calculating the mean silhouette coefficient provides for a simple scoring method of a given clustering.

Answer:
No, changing delivery services will not affect all customers equally, since, as our segments show, some customers rely on the wholesaler for much larger sized shipments than others, as well as for different kinds of goods than others, which means that they would be affected to different extents and in different ways.


We could use the structure in this data, as uncovered by our clustering, to run an A/B test that splits its control and variation groups equally among the two segments, making sure that the differences captured in the A/B test are not skewed due to unbalanced representations of the two segments. By having the A and B segments of the test labeled as members of segments 0 and 1, and by having these segments represented equally in the two groups tested, the wholesaler can then examine both the overall effect of the shipping change by examining the behaviour of group A vs B, and also the targeted effect of the change on each of the two segments, by viewing them in isolation, thus determining which of the two is more affected by the change.
